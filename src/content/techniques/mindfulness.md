Mindfulness is not about emptying your mind or achieving a permanent state of calm. It's about learning to be present with whatever is actually happening — including the uncomfortable parts — without automatically reacting.

**What this means in practice:**

We'll weave mindfulness into our work together in ways that fit your life. This might include brief in-session practices, body awareness exercises, or simply learning to notice when you've been pulled into a worry spiral or a self-critical story. The goal is to create a little space — enough space to choose your response rather than just react.

Research from fields as diverse as neuroscience, clinical psychology, and medicine consistently shows that regular mindfulness practice changes the brain in measurable ways: reducing the reactivity of the amygdala, strengthening the prefrontal cortex's capacity for regulation, and lowering baseline levels of cortisol (the stress hormone).

Mindfulness-based approaches I draw from include Mindfulness-Based Cognitive Therapy (MBCT), Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (MBSR), and mindfulness elements within ACT and DBT.

**Good for:** anxiety and chronic stress, depression relapse prevention, emotional reactivity, insomnia, chronic pain, burnout, wanting to feel more present in daily life
